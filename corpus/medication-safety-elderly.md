# Medication Safety in Older Adults — Guideline Summary

Educational summary of common geriatric-care guidance (Beers-criteria-style) for the CareSwarm local corpus.

## Core principles
- Keep one complete, current list of EVERYTHING taken: prescriptions, supplements, traditional remedies. Bring it to every appointment.
- One pharmacy where possible — interaction checking works best with full visibility.
- Any new symptom in an older adult is a medication side effect until proven otherwise (dizziness, confusion, falls, constipation, poor appetite are classic drug effects).

## High-risk situations
- **Polypharmacy** (≥5 medications): ask the doctor yearly to review and deprescribe what is no longer needed.
- **Sedatives/sleep medications** (benzodiazepines, "Z-drugs"): double fall and fracture risk; avoid as long-term sleep solutions.
- **NSAIDs** (ibuprofen, diclofenac): stomach bleeding and kidney strain, especially with blood-pressure medication; prefer paracetamol for routine pain.
- **Anticoagulants** (warfarin, DOACs): report any unusual bruising, pink urine, black stools immediately; never double a missed dose.
- **Diabetes medications**: hypoglycemia presents as confusion in the elderly — often mistaken for dementia.

## Practical adherence
Weekly pill organizer filled on the same day each week; alarms tied to meals; mark refill dates on the calendar 1 week early. If swallowing is hard, ask the pharmacist before crushing — many tablets must not be crushed (slow-release).

## Red flags needing prompt review
New confusion or drowsiness after a medication change; a fall; dizziness on standing (check blood pressure sitting AND standing); appetite loss lasting more than a few days.
