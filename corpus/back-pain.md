# Low Back Pain — Guideline Summary

Educational summary of common primary-care guidance for the CareSwarm local corpus.

## The reassuring majority
90% of acute low back pain is "non-specific" (muscular/mechanical) and improves substantially within 2–6 weeks. Imaging (X-ray/MRI) is NOT recommended early without red flags — incidental findings mislead more than they help.

## What actually works
- **Stay active.** Bed rest beyond 1–2 days delays recovery. Continue daily activities within pain tolerance.
- Heat packs 15–20 minutes; gentle walking several times daily; gradual return to lifting with hip-hinge technique.
- Paracetamol or short-course NSAIDs per label if needed (NSAIDs with food; caution with stomach/kidney/blood-pressure issues).
- After 2 weeks: structured exercise (core strengthening, swimming, yoga) prevents recurrence better than any passive treatment.

## See a doctor within days
- Pain radiating below the knee with numbness/tingling (sciatica pattern) lasting more than 1–2 weeks.
- No improvement at all after 4 weeks of self-care.
- Night pain not relieved by changing position, unexplained weight loss, history of cancer, fever, or age over 50 with new pain — earlier review.

## Emergency criteria (cauda equina / serious pathology)
- New loss of bladder or bowel control, or unable to pass urine.
- Numbness in the saddle/groin area.
- Progressive leg weakness (foot drop, can't rise on toes).
- Severe pain after significant trauma, or in anyone with osteoporosis/steroid use (possible fracture).
