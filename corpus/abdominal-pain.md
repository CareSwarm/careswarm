# Abdominal Pain — Assessment Guideline Summary

Educational summary of common primary-care guidance for the CareSwarm local corpus.

## Location gives clues (not diagnosis)
- Upper middle (epigastric), burning, food-related: gastritis/reflux/ulcer.
- Right upper, after fatty meals, radiating to right shoulder: gallbladder.
- Around navel migrating to right lower over hours, with fever and loss of appetite: classic appendicitis pattern — urgent.
- Lower abdomen with painful/frequent urination: urinary tract infection.
- Crampy, diffuse, with diarrhea after suspect food: gastroenteritis — usually self-limited 1–3 days.

## Home care for mild, familiar pain
Small bland meals, oral rehydration for diarrhea/vomiting, avoid NSAIDs (ibuprofen/aspirin) on an irritated stomach — paracetamol is safer for pain. Antacids per label for burning pain.

## See a doctor within 24–48 hours
- Pain persisting more than 2–3 days or steadily worsening.
- Recurrent burning pain relieved by food then returning (ulcer pattern) — test for H. pylori.
- Diarrhea more than 3 days, or with blood/mucus.
- Unintentional weight loss, black stools, or difficulty swallowing — prompt evaluation.

## Emergency criteria
- Severe pain that wakes from sleep or makes the abdomen rigid/board-like to touch.
- Pain with persistent vomiting (can't keep fluids down 12+ hours) or vomiting blood.
- Right-lower pain with fever (possible appendicitis); severe pain in pregnancy.
- No bowel movement or gas with bloating and vomiting (possible obstruction).
- Black tarry stools with dizziness or fainting (GI bleeding).
