# Cough and Respiratory Infections — Guideline Summary

Educational summary of common primary-care guidance for the CareSwarm local corpus.

## Typical course
Acute cough from a viral upper respiratory infection lasts up to 3 weeks; improvement is usually evident by day 7–10. Colored sputum alone does NOT mean bacterial infection or antibiotics.

## Home care
Fluids, rest, honey (1–2 teaspoons, adults and children over 1 year — never under 1 year), warm saline gargles, humidified air. Decongestants short-term only (≤5 days). Stop smoking exposure — it doubles recovery time.

## See a doctor within 24–48 hours
- Fever >38.5°C lasting more than 3 days, or fever returning after improvement ("double sickening" — suspicious for pneumonia).
- Cough with chest pain on breathing, or breathlessness on light activity.
- Wheeze not responding to a usual inhaler; new wheeze in an adult.
- Cough lasting more than 3 weeks (consider TB testing in endemic areas — free national TB programs exist in Vietnam).
- Coughing blood (any amount) — same-day review.

## Emergency criteria
- Struggling to breathe at rest: can't finish sentences, ribs pulling in (children), blue lips.
- Drowsiness or confusion with breathing difficulty.
- High fever with fast breathing (>30/min adult) and chest pain.

## Antibiotic stewardship
Most bronchitis is viral. Antibiotics are reserved for likely pneumonia, confirmed strep throat, whooping cough, or high-risk patients — a clinician's call after examination. Never reuse leftover antibiotics.
