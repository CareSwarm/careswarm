# Exertional Chest Pain (Angina) — Guideline Summary

Derived from public WHO/AHA patient guidance. Educational summary for the CareSwarm local corpus.

## Pattern recognition
Chest pain or tightness that appears during physical effort (climbing stairs, brisk walking, carrying loads) and eases within minutes of rest is the classic pattern of **stable angina** — reduced blood flow to the heart muscle from narrowed coronary arteries. Typical descriptions: pressure, squeezing, heaviness, band-like tightness; may radiate to the left arm, jaw, neck, or back.

## Risk factors that raise concern
Age over 55 (men) or 65 (women), smoking, hypertension, diabetes, high cholesterol, family history of early heart disease, obesity, sedentary lifestyle. New exertional chest tightness in a person over 60 with any risk factor warrants medical evaluation within days, not weeks.

## What stable angina is NOT
- Pain that starts at rest, lasts more than 15–20 minutes, or wakes the person at night suggests **unstable angina or myocardial infarction** — an emergency.
- Sharp pain that changes with breathing or position is more often musculoskeletal or pleuritic.
- Burning pain after meals, worse lying down, suggests reflux — but cardiac causes must be excluded first in older adults.

## Recommended actions
1. Stop activity and rest at first symptoms; note duration and triggers.
2. Book a medical review promptly (within 1 week) for new exertional symptoms; sooner if episodes become more frequent, longer, or occur with less effort (crescendo pattern — treat as urgent).
3. Expect the clinician to consider: ECG, blood tests (lipids, glucose), exercise stress testing.
4. Do not start or stop cardiac medication without professional advice.

## Emergency criteria
Call emergency services immediately if chest pain: lasts >15 minutes at rest; comes with sweating, nausea, breathlessness, fainting; radiates to jaw/arm with severe intensity; or occurs with collapse.
